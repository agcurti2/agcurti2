Overview Back to top ↑

In this work, I develop a set of distributed control algorithms to facilitate swarm shape formation that is persistent, meaning the shapes can last beyond the energy duration of individual robots, and adaptable to formation changes in real time. The idea is that a swarm can persist in a shape perpetually, and then that shape can be sculpted into a new shape by a human in the same way an artist may sculpt clay.

Shape Persistence Back to top ↑

Shape persistence is achieved by finding Hamiltonian cycles through 2D shapes so that robots can follow a Hamiltonian path through a shape that both approximates the shape and moves robots through the shape in a first-in-first-out order. This means that robots are guaranteed to enter the shape, travel through the shape without collision, and then exit the shape so that they can return to a charging station. Therefore, individual robots are continuously recharging and reentering the shape, while the swarm maintains the shape as a whole persistently and indefinitely. In the paper, it is proven that a Hamiltonian cycle can be found for any valid, continuous 2D shape.

Shape Adaptability Back to top ↑

Shape adaptability is achieved by detecting the change (in this case a human gesture), filling in a new part of the shape (or removing a part of the shape), and then finding a new Hamiltonian cycle through the new shape. Finding the new Hamiltonian cycle is done using a mix of robot motion and peer-to-peer communication. Like shape persistence, it can be proven that a new Hamiltonian cycle can be found for any valid addition or any valid subtraction to the 2D shape.

Demonstration Back to top ↑

The 2D shape formation algorithms were demonstrated on a swarm of differential drive ground robots called Coachbots. The swarm maintained a shape in the middle of the arena while cycling robots to a charging rail at the edge of the arena.
